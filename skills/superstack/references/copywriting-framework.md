## 1. Research Process

### Step 1: Understand the product
Ask the user (and research online):
- What does this product/service actually do? (In plain language, not marketing speak)
- What's the origin story? Why was it created?
- What makes it different from alternatives?
- What's the pricing model?
- Who are the happiest customers, and why?

### Step 2: Map the competitive landscape
Search for 3-5 direct competitors. For each, note:
- Their headline and positioning
- What benefits they emphasize
- What language/tone they use
- What they don't mention (gaps = opportunities)
- Their pricing and social proof approach

### Step 3: Identify the target audience
Go beyond demographics. Build a profile:
- **Job/role**: What do they do day-to-day?
- **Frustration**: What problem makes them search for a solution?
- **Current solution**: What are they doing now? (Spreadsheets? A competitor? Nothing?)
- **Decision factors**: What would make them switch? Price? Features? Ease of use?
- **Language**: How do they describe their problem? (Use their words, not yours)

### Step 4: Search for current best practices
Copywriting trends evolve. Search the web for current conversion copywriting best practices, landing page copy frameworks, and any recent shifts in how high-performing sites structure their messaging. This keeps the output fresh and effective.

## 2. Pain Point Mapping

Map 3-5 core pain points. For each:

| Pain Point | Emotional Impact | Current Workaround | How We Solve It | Proof |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| e.g., "Losing customers without knowing why" | Anxiety, helplessness | Manually checking spreadsheets | Automated churn prediction dashboard | "Reduced churn by 34% in 60 days" |

This table drives the entire page structure. Each pain point becomes a section on the page.

## 3. Headline Formulas

### The Transformation Formula
**[End state] without [obstacle/pain]**
- "Scale your team without the hiring chaos"
- "Ship features weekly without breaking production"

### The Specific Outcome Formula
**[Specific result] in [timeframe]**
- "Go from idea to deployed SaaS in a weekend"
- "Cut your support tickets by half in 30 days"

### The Question Formula
**What if [dream scenario]?**
- "What if your landing page actually converted?"
- "What if onboarding took 2 minutes, not 2 weeks?"

### The Contrast Formula
**Stop [pain]. Start [gain].**
- "Stop guessing what your users want. Start knowing."
- "Stop drowning in spreadsheets. Start making decisions."

### The Authority Formula
**The [tool/approach] that [impressive result]**
- "The analytics platform that 500+ SaaS founders trust"
- "The deployment pipeline that handles 10M requests/day"

### Rules for headlines
- Be specific over clever. Numbers, timeframes, and percentages outperform vague claims.
- Match the awareness level. If the audience knows their problem, name it. If they don't, educate first.
- Test the "so what?" reaction. Read your headline aloud — if someone could say "so what?", rewrite it.

## 4. Section-by-Section Writing Guide

### Hero Section
The hero has ~3 seconds to communicate value. Structure:
1. **Headline**: The transformation promise (see formulas above)
2. **Subheadline**: 1-2 sentences expanding on how. More detail, still benefit-focused.
3. **Primary CTA**: Action-oriented, specific to the next step
4. **Social proof snippet**: One line — "Trusted by 2,000+ teams" or a recognizable logo bar
5. **Visual**: Product screenshot, demo animation, or abstract brand visual — NOT a generic stock photo

### Problem Section
Right after the hero, validate the visitor's pain. This builds "they get me" trust.
- Describe the problem in the user's language
- Use the PAS framework: Pain → Agitation (consequences of not solving) → Solution (transition to your product)
- Keep it empathetic, not condescending

### Solution/Features Section
Now show how you solve it. Key principles:
- Lead with benefits, follow with features. Not "AI-powered analytics" but "Know exactly which customers are about to leave — before they do."
- Show, don't tell. Screenshots, GIFs, short videos, interactive demos.
- Group features by use case or user journey, not by technical category.

### Social Proof Section
Types of social proof (use at least 2):
- **Testimonials**: Real quotes with name, photo, role, company. Specific results beat generic praise.
- **Logo bar**: Recognizable company logos. Even 3-5 logos add credibility.
- **Case study snippets**: "[Company] reduced [metric] by [amount] in [time]"
- **Numbers**: "10,000+ users", "$2M saved", "99.9% uptime"

### How It Works Section
3-4 steps, dead simple:
1. Sign up (30 seconds)
2. Connect your data
3. Get insights on your dashboard

Each step: one icon, one headline, one sentence. Remove all friction from the mental model.

### Pricing Section (if applicable)
- Lead with value, not price
- Anchor the most popular plan visually
- Use feature comparison for plan differentiation
- Address the "is it worth it?" objection with ROI framing
- Include money-back guarantee or free trial to reduce risk

### FAQ Section
Address the top 5-7 objections:
- "Is my data secure?"
- "Can I cancel anytime?"
- "How long does setup take?"
- "Do you integrate with [tool]?"
- "What if I need help?"

Write answers that are direct, confident, and brief. No corporate hedging.

### Final CTA Section
Repeat the core value proposition. Restate the transformation. One more CTA. Add urgency if appropriate ("Start your free trial — no credit card required").

## 5. CTA Strategy

### CTA hierarchy
- **Primary**: The main action (Sign up, Start free trial, Get a demo)
- **Secondary**: Lower commitment alternative (Watch a video, Read the case study)
- **Tertiary**: Minimal commitment (Join the newsletter, Follow us)

### CTA copywriting rules
- Use first person: "Start my free trial" outperforms "Start your free trial"
- Be specific: "See my dashboard" beats "Get started"
- Reduce perceived effort: "Takes 30 seconds" or "No credit card required"
- Match the section's context: After a testimonial → "Join 2,000+ happy teams". After features → "Try it yourself"

## 6. Tone Calibration

The tone should match the brand and audience. Ask the user where they fall on these spectrums:

- **Formal ←→ Casual**: "Our solution optimizes..." vs. "We help you..."
- **Technical ←→ Simple**: "ML-powered predictive churn model" vs. "Knows when customers are about to leave"
- **Bold ←→ Measured**: "The only tool you'll ever need" vs. "A reliable addition to your toolkit"
- **Playful ←→ Serious**: Humor and personality vs. trust and authority

Default: Conversational, confident, specific. Not corporate, not slangy.

## 7. Common Mistakes to Avoid

- **Feature-first copy**: "We have AI, dashboards, and integrations" → Nobody cares about features. They care about what features do for them.
- **Vague claims**: "Best-in-class", "cutting-edge", "innovative" → These are empty. Replace with specifics.
- **Too much text**: A landing page is not a whitepaper. Every word must earn its place.
- **Ignoring objections**: If you don't address "Is it secure?" on the page, visitors will assume the answer is no.
- **Generic CTAs**: "Submit", "Learn More", "Get Started" → These are missed opportunities for persuasion.
- **Inconsistent tone**: Don't mix corporate formality with casual slang. Pick a voice and stick to it.
- **Missing social proof**: Even early-stage products can use founder credibility, beta tester quotes, or "as seen in" mentions.
